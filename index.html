On the Properties of Feature Attribution for Supervised Contrastive Learning

Arrighi, Belloni, Gallet, Gentile, Lippi, Zullich

Two image classifiers can reach the same accuracy yet "explain" themselves very differently. We show that the training objective — not just the network — shapes the quality of the saliency maps used to interpret a model. Networks trained with supervised contrastive learning (SCL) produce explanations that are more faithful, more stable, and more focused than the standard cross-entropy baseline, because contrastive training organizes the internal feature space more cleanly.

Scene 1

The Finding

Take one backbone and train it three ways — cross-entropy (CE), supervised contrastive loss (SCL), and triplet loss (TL) — until all three hit comparable accuracy. When you then ask each model "where did you look?", the answers differ systematically. The objective you optimized leaves a fingerprint on the explanation, and SCL's fingerprint is consistently the cleanest.

Scene 2

The Mechanism

Why would the loss change the explanations? Contrastive training explicitly pulls same-class examples together and pushes different classes apart, building a tightly clustered embedding space. That well-structured geometry gives the gradient a cleaner signal to attribute back to the input.

The result is saliency that lands on the object rather than scattering across the background — a direct, geometric reason for the quality gap, not a coincidence of tuning.

Scene 3

The Scoreboard

Scored with Grad-CAM across both datasets, SCL wins three of the four sharply-defined metrics: faithfulness, continuity, and complexity. The wins hold on small images (CIFAR10) and on harder, higher-resolution scenes (ImageNet-S50) alike.

Crucially, this is not an accuracy story: all three objectives reach within roughly a point of each other, so the explanation gap is attributable to the loss itself.

Scene 4

The Facets

"Good explanation" is not one thing. We evaluate five Co-12 facets — faithfulness, coherence, continuity, contrastivity, and complexity — each capturing a different property a trustworthy saliency map should have.

Splitting the question this way is what lets us be honest: SCL does not win everywhere, and naming the facets makes precise exactly where it leads and where it does not.

Scene 5

It Generalizes

The pattern is not an artifact of one tiny dataset. Moving from CIFAR10 to ImageNet-S50 — larger images, fifty classes, real-world clutter — the same ranking holds, and some gaps widen (for example, continuity on ImageNet-S50 jumps from 0.38 for CE to 0.61 for SCL).

A different attribution tool tells the same story: Eigen-CAM continuity on CIFAR10 climbs 0.49 → 0.69 → 0.80 across CE, SCL, and TL. The effect is robust to both dataset and explainer.

What we do NOT claim

  • SCL is not universally best. On contrastivity (do maps for different classes actually differ?), cross-entropy leads on ImageNet-S50 (CE 0.20 vs SCL 0.34, lower is better) and triplet loss leads on CIFAR10. We report this openly rather than averaging it away.
  • Coherence is inconclusive. Overlap with human ground-truth segmentation (ImageNet-S50) shows no reliable winner: PointingGame CE 0.84 vs SCL 0.85, Attribution-Localization CE 0.63 vs SCL 0.68, but the run-to-run variance is so large (std up to ±0.37) that no objective can be declared the winner.
  • We do not claim a causal proof. The embedding-geometry mechanism is a strongly supported explanation for the observed differences, not a formal causal guarantee.
